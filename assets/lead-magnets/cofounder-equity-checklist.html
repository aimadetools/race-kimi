The Co-Founder Equity Checklist

A step-by-step guide to splitting equity fairly, protecting your IP, and avoiding the disputes that kill startups.

⚠️ Why this matters 65% of startup failures trace back to co-founder conflict. The #1 cause? Equity disagreements that could have been prevented with a transparent conversation upfront. Use this checklist before you incorporate, before you raise money, and before resentment has a chance to grow.
1 Have the Hard Conversation First
  • Discuss expectations openly. How many hours per week will each founder work? For how long?
  • Agree on roles and responsibilities. Who is CEO? Who owns product, engineering, sales, ops?
  • Talk about cash contributions. Will anyone invest personal savings? How much? When?
  • Disclose prior IP and side projects. What code, designs, patents, or relationships are being brought in?
  • Set a decision-making framework. What happens when you disagree? Tie-breaker rules prevent deadlock.
Notes:
2 Quantify Each Founder's Contribution
  • Time commitment: Hours per week × weeks committed × fair market rate
  • Cash investment: Money invested into the company (not expenses). Typically weighted 3-4× vs time.
  • IP and prior work: Code, designs, patents, data, relationships brought into the company
  • Experience and network: Domain expertise, previous exits, valuable connections
  • Use a calculator. Run the numbers together at fairsplit.co to remove emotion from the decision.
Notes:
3 Protect Everyone with Vesting
  • Set a vesting schedule. Standard: 4 years with a 1-year cliff. Shorter for late-stage founders.
  • Define what happens if someone leaves early. Good leaver vs bad leaver provisions protect both sides.
  • Consider acceleration clauses. Single-trigger (acquisition) and double-trigger (acquisition + termination).
  • File 83(b) elections within 30 days. Missing this window can cost founders tens of thousands in taxes.
  • Use a vesting calculator. Model your schedule month-by-month at fairsplit.co/vesting-calculator
Notes:
4 Lock Down IP Ownership
  • List all pre-existing IP. Document what each founder owned before the company started.
  • Assign future IP to the company. Everything created for the business should belong to the entity, not individuals.
  • Disclose third-party components. Open-source libraries, licensed assets, contractor work — list them all.
  • Get IP assignments in writing. Verbal agreements are worthless to investors and acquirers.
  • Consider moral rights waivers. Important if you have international founders or operate globally.
Notes:
5 Document Everything in a Signed Agreement
  • Co-Founder Agreement: Equity split, roles, vesting, decision-making, termination, dispute resolution
  • Vesting Agreement: Detailed schedule, repurchase terms, acceleration, 83(b) instructions
  • IP Assignment Agreement: Pre-existing IP disclosure, assignment of future work, third-party disclosures
  • All founders sign before any major milestone. Before fundraising, before hiring, before launching — ideally before writing the first line of code together.
  • Have an attorney review. Templates get you 90% there. A 1-hour lawyer review gets you to 100%.
Notes:
6 Plan for the Future
  • Set a review date. Revisit the agreement annually or after any major change (new founder, funding, pivot).
  • Keep originals in a safe place. Signed PDFs, cap table, 83(b) filings — store with your company records.
  • Share with your lawyer and accountant. They need to know the structure for future advice and filings.
  • Update when things change. New co-founders, funding rounds, or role shifts should trigger an amendment.
