Changelog Widgets vs Static Pages: Which Is Right for You?

A detailed comparison to help you choose the best approach for announcing product updates to your users.

When it comes to announcing product updates, SaaS companies have two main options: embeddable changelog widgets or dedicated static pages. Both approaches have their place, but the right choice depends on your specific needs, audience, and resources.

In this guide, we'll break down the pros and cons of each approach, look at real-world examples, and help you decide which fits your product best.

What's the Difference?

Before diving into the comparison, let's clarify what we mean by each term:

Changelog Widgets are embeddable components that appear within your application. Think of the bell icon in the corner of many SaaS apps that opens a slide-out panel showing recent updates. These are typically JavaScript-based and integrate directly into your product's UI.

Static Changelog Pages are standalone web pages (like yoursite.com/changelog) that live outside your application. Users navigate to these pages to see updates, or they're linked from your marketing site, emails, or in-app navigation.

🔔 Changelog Widgets

Embeddable components that appear inside your application

Pros

  • Immediate visibility to active users
  • Contextual (seen while using product)
  • Higher engagement rates
  • Can show unread badges
  • Works for logged-in users

Cons

  • Adds JavaScript payload to your app
  • Requires engineering time to implement
  • Only visible to logged-in users
  • Can feel intrusive if overused
  • Typically more expensive ($49+/month)

📄 Static Pages

Standalone pages accessible via URL

Pros

  • Fast loading (just HTML/CSS)
  • SEO benefits (indexable content)
  • Accessible to everyone
  • Easy to share and link
  • More affordable ($12/month)

Cons

  • Users must actively visit
  • Lower immediate visibility
  • No unread notifications
  • Requires driving traffic
  • Less contextual

Deep Dive: When to Choose Each

Choose a Widget If:

1. You have high user engagement within your app

If users spend significant time in your application (think project management tools, design software, dashboards), widgets make sense. The update is visible right where users work.

2. You need to announce critical updates immediately

Security patches, breaking changes, or downtime notifications need immediate attention. Widgets put this information front and center.

3. Your changelog is a core product feature

For some products (especially developer tools), the changelog IS a feature. Users actively want to know what's new every time they log in.

4. You have engineering resources

Implementing and maintaining a widget requires developer time. If you have a dedicated frontend team, this isn't an issue. For solo founders, it might be a burden.

Choose a Static Page If:

1. SEO matters to your growth strategy

Static pages are fully indexable by search engines. Every update is a new opportunity to rank for keywords. Companies like Linear and Notion get significant organic traffic from their changelogs.

2. You want to reach prospects, not just users

A public changelog shows prospects that you're actively improving. It's social proof of a living, evolving product. Widgets only reach existing users.

3. You prefer simplicity

Static pages don't add JavaScript to your app, don't require maintenance, and won't break if the third-party service has issues. Set it and forget it.

4. Budget is a concern

Widget-based solutions like Beamer or Headway start at $49-99/month. Static page solutions like Changelog.page are $12/month or less.

The Hybrid Approach

Here's a secret: you don't have to choose just one.

Many successful SaaS companies use both:

  • Static page as the primary source of truth, optimized for SEO and sharing
  • In-app badge/link that points to the static page
  • Email digest summarizing recent updates

This approach gives you the SEO benefits and accessibility of static pages, while still making updates visible to active users through strategic placement of links or simple notification badges.

For example: Add a "What's New" link in your app navigation that opens your changelog in a new tab. Simple, effective, no JavaScript widget required.

Real-World Examples

Companies Using Widgets:

Intercom — Uses an in-app widget for product announcements, but also maintains a public changelog page. The widget is for immediate announcements; the page is for the full history.

Vercel — Has a "What's New" section in their dashboard, but their detailed changelog is a standalone page that's heavily SEO-optimized.

Companies Using Static Pages:

Linear — Their changelog at linear.app/changelog is a masterpiece of static page design. It's become a significant traffic driver and conversion tool.

Notion — Uses a public changelog page that showcases new features with rich media. It's both documentation and marketing.

Stripe — Their changelog is a reference implementation of a well-organized static page with filtering, search, and RSS feeds.

Our Recommendation

For most SaaS companies, especially those in the $0-$10M ARR range, we recommend starting with a static page for these reasons:

  1. SEO value compounds over time. Every update is content that can rank.
  2. Lower total cost of ownership. No engineering time, lower monthly cost.
  3. Maximum reach. Prospects, users, investors, and journalists can all access it.
  4. Flexibility. You can always add a widget later; the static page remains your source of truth.

If you later find that users are missing critical updates, add a simple "What's New" badge in your app that links to your static changelog. This hybrid approach gives you 80% of the widget benefit with 20% of the cost.

Key Takeaways

  • Widgets excel at in-app visibility but add complexity and cost
  • Static pages offer SEO benefits and broader reach at lower cost
  • The hybrid approach (static page + simple in-app link) often gives the best of both worlds
  • Start with a static page and add widgets only if you have a specific use case
  • Consider your audience: Widgets reach users; static pages reach everyone

The best changelog is the one you'll actually maintain. Choose the approach that fits your workflow, resources, and audience — not the one with the most features.
