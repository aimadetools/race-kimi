The Only Fair Way to Split Equity With a Co-Founder

The most common mistake new founders make is splitting equity 50/50. It feels safe. It feels friendly. But it ignores reality.

What if one founder quits their job to work full-time while the other keeps a day job? What if one founder invests $20,000 while the other invests $0? What if one brings years of industry experience and a network, while the other is learning on the job?

A 50/50 split pretends these differences don't exist. Over time, resentment builds. The founder doing more work starts to feel cheated. Conversations get awkward. In the worst cases, the partnership collapses and the startup dies.

The contribution-based alternative

A better approach is to split equity based on what each founder actually contributes. At FairSplit, we use a simple weighted model:

  • Time: Count the hours each founder works and multiply by a fair market hourly rate. Adjust for experience level.
  • Cash: Value cash contributions at a higher multiplier (usually 4x) because cash is more scarce and risky than time.
  • IP and prior work: If a founder built the prototype, filed patents, or brought valuable code, assign a fair market value to that contribution.

Add up each founder's weighted contributions, then divide by the total. That's your fair split.

Why this works

This method isn't perfect, but it has one huge advantage: it's transparent. Both founders can see exactly how the numbers are calculated. It turns a subjective, emotional conversation into an objective, rational one.

And if circumstances change later? That's what vesting is for. (We'll cover vesting in the next post.)

Vesting Schedules Explained for First-Time Founders

You and your co-founder agree on a fair equity split. Great. Now what happens if one of you leaves after three months?

Without vesting, the departing founder keeps their full ownership forever—even if they stopped contributing. That's unfair to the founder who stays and does the hard work of building the company.

What is vesting?

Vesting means you earn your equity over time instead of getting it all upfront. The standard startup vesting schedule is:

  • 4-year vesting: You earn your full equity over four years.
  • 1-year cliff: If you leave before the first year, you get nothing.
  • Monthly vesting after the cliff: After year one, you earn 1/48 of your equity each month.

Why the cliff matters

The one-year cliff protects the company from a founder who joins, does almost nothing, and leaves with a chunk of ownership. It also gives both founders a full year to prove the partnership works.

Single-trigger vs. double-trigger acceleration

Some agreements include "acceleration" clauses that speed up vesting if the company is acquired. Single-trigger acceleration means vesting accelerates on any acquisition. Double-trigger means it only accelerates if you're fired or demoted after the acquisition.

Most investors prefer double-trigger because it keeps founders motivated to stay after an acquisition.

IP Assignment: Why Every Co-Founder Needs One

Here's a nightmare scenario: two founders build a SaaS together. One writes all the code. The other designs the product and markets it. They never sign an IP assignment agreement.

A year later, the technical founder leaves—and takes the code with them. Or worse, a competitor claims the departing founder is infringing their rights by working on the product.

Without a clear IP assignment, the company might not legally own the very thing it's trying to sell.

What does an IP assignment do?

An IP assignment agreement is a contract where founders agree that any intellectual property they create for the company belongs to the company. This includes:

  • Code and software
  • Designs and branding
  • Product ideas and business plans
  • Patents and trademarks

When should you sign it?

Before you start working together. If you've already been working for a while, sign it retroactively as soon as possible. The longer you wait, the riskier it gets.

Do you really need a lawyer?

For most early-stage startups, a standard IP assignment template is enough to get started. Our template is attorney-reviewed and covers the essential language you need. As you raise funding or expand internationally, you'll want a lawyer to customize it. But don't let the absence of a $500/hour attorney stop you from protecting your company today.

What Happens When a Co-Founder Leaves: A Complete Guide to Founder Departures

One of the hardest conversations in startup life isn't about starting—it's about ending. When a co-founder leaves, emotions run high, and the financial stakes are enormous. Without proper planning, a departing founder can walk away with a chunk of your company that they no longer contribute to, leaving the remaining team demoralized and investors wary.

The nightmare scenario

Imagine this: You and your co-founder start a company with a 50/50 split. Six months in, your co-founder gets a dream job offer at Google and leaves. You continue working nights and weekends, sacrificing weekends and savings to keep the dream alive. Two years later, you raise a seed round—and your departed co-founder still owns 50% of a company they haven't thought about in 18 months.

This happens more often than you'd think. And it's entirely preventable.

How vesting protects the company

Vesting is your first line of defense. With standard 4-year vesting and a 1-year cliff, here's what happens when a founder leaves:

  • Leaves before 12 months: Gets nothing. The cliff means zero equity until the first anniversary.
  • Leaves at 18 months: Keeps 37.5% of their allocated equity (18/48 months vested). The unvested 62.5% returns to the company.
  • Leaves at 3 years: Keeps 75% of their allocated equity (36/48 months vested).
  • Leaves after 4 years: Keeps 100%—they've earned it.

This aligns incentives perfectly. Founders who stay and build get rewarded. Founders who leave early don't walk away with unfair ownership.

What happens to the unvested equity?

When a founder departs, their unvested shares typically revert to the company. The remaining founders can then:

  • Redistribute them to existing founders who are picking up the slack
  • Allocate them to a replacement co-founder
  • Hold them in reserve for future hires or investors

The importance of a written agreement

Vesting terms should be spelled out in your co-founder agreement before anyone writes a line of code. The agreement should cover:

  • Vesting schedule (4 years / 1-year cliff)
  • What happens to unvested shares on departure
  • Company repurchase rights for vested shares
  • Good leaver vs bad leaver provisions
  • IP assignment (ensuring departing founder can't take the code)

Good leaver vs bad leaver

Many agreements distinguish between "good leaver" and "bad leaver" scenarios:

  • Good leaver: Leaves for personal reasons, health issues, or by mutual agreement. May keep vested shares or get accelerated vesting.
  • Bad leaver: Terminated for cause, breach of contract, or joins a competitor. May forfeit some or all vested shares.

Managing the departure process

When a co-founder leaves, follow these steps:

  1. Document everything: Get written confirmation of resignation and last day.
  2. Calculate vested shares: Determine exact ownership based on vesting schedule.
  3. Update cap table: Adjust ownership percentages in your records.
  4. Transfer IP: Ensure all code, accounts, and company assets are transferred.
  5. Notify stakeholders: Inform investors, advisors, and key team members.
  6. Update agreements: Remove departed founder from bank accounts, legal documents, and contracts.

Sweat Equity vs Cash Investment: How to Value Different Founder Contributions

When two or more founders start a company, they rarely contribute equally. One might put in $50,000 of savings while another works full-time without pay. One brings a patented invention while another contributes industry connections and expertise. How do you fairly compare these different types of contributions when splitting equity?

The fundamental challenge

Cash, time, and intellectual property are not directly comparable. A dollar of cash is concrete and immediate. An hour of work is variable in quality and uncertain in value. A patent might be worth millions or nothing depending on market conditions.

Yet founders must find a way to value each contribution type to arrive at a fair equity split. Here's how sophisticated founders do it.

Valuing sweat equity (time contributions)

The standard approach is to value each founder's time at their market rate—the salary they could earn elsewhere. Then apply a discount for the risk they're taking:

  • Market rate: What would this person earn as an employee? (e.g., $150K/year for a senior engineer)
  • Hours worked: Track actual hours contributed monthly
  • Risk multiplier: Multiply by 1.5-2x to account for the risk of joining a startup

Example: A technical founder works 6 months full-time. Market rate: $12,500/month. With 1.5x risk multiplier: $12,500 × 6 × 1.5 = $112,500 contribution value.

Valuing cash investments

Cash is more valuable than time because:

  • It's immediately available and liquid
  • It can't be taken back (unlike unvested time)
  • It often comes from personal savings, representing real sacrifice
  • It's essential for paying contractors, servers, and other hard costs

The standard approach is to value cash at a 2-4x multiple of time. So $10,000 of cash might equal $20,000-$40,000 of sweat equity value.

The contribution multiplier framework

Here's a practical framework for weighting different contribution types:

Contribution Type Multiplier Notes
Cash 4x Scarce, irreversible, immediate value
Existing IP/Patents 3x Pre-built assets with proven value
Full-time work 1.5x Market rate with risk premium
Part-time work 1.0x Market rate, no premium (lower risk)
Industry connections Case-by-case Value based on actual introductions made

Common pitfalls to avoid

Pitfall #1: Valuing future promises

Don't count promised contributions that haven't happened yet. A founder saying "I'll introduce you to investors" or "I'll work full-time next month" shouldn't count until it actually happens.

Pitfall #2: Ignoring opportunity cost differences

A founder quitting a $300K/year Google job is taking more risk than one leaving a $60K/year startup. Their contributions should be weighted differently even if hours are the same.

Pitfall #3: Treating all cash equally

Personal savings invested should be valued higher than a loan from parents, which should be valued higher than a company credit card advance.

Dynamic recalculation

Smart founders don't just calculate once at the start. They revisit the numbers quarterly during the first year as actual contributions become clear. A founder who promised full-time effort but only delivers part-time should see their equity adjusted accordingly.

This is where vesting becomes crucial—it gives you the mechanism to adjust for reality.
