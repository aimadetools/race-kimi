Notion has become the go-to workspace for startups and product teams. Its flexibility makes it a popular choice for creating internal documentation, wikis, and yes—even product changelogs.

In this guide, we'll walk through exactly how to create a professional changelog in Notion, complete with templates and examples. We'll also discuss when it makes sense to stick with Notion versus upgrading to a dedicated changelog tool.

Why Use Notion for Changelogs?

Before diving into the setup, let's understand why teams choose Notion for their changelogs:

  • Familiar workflow: Your team already lives in Notion
  • Quick to set up: No new tools to learn or configure
  • Flexible structure: Databases, views, and properties adapt to your needs
  • Internal focus: Perfect for team-only changelogs
  • Free to start: No immediate cost for small teams
💡 Best for:

Notion works best for internal changelogs—updates meant for your team rather than customers. For public-facing changelogs, you may eventually want a dedicated tool (more on that later).

Setting Up Your Changelog Database

The foundation of a good Notion changelog is a well-structured database. Here's how to set it up:

1

Create a New Database

In your Notion workspace, create a new page and select Table as the database type. Name it "Product Changelog" or whatever makes sense for your team.

2

Add Essential Properties

Set up these properties to organize your entries:

  • Title (Title) - Name of the update
  • Date (Date) - When the change went live
  • Category (Select) - Feature, Fix, Improvement, Security
  • Status (Select) - Draft, Published, Archived
  • Author (Person) - Who made the update
  • Description (Text) - Brief summary
3

Create Useful Views

Set up multiple views for different use cases:

  • All Entries - Complete changelog history
  • By Category - Grouped view by type of change
  • Recent Updates - Filtered to last 30 days
  • Drafts - Unpublished entries only

Writing Tips for Notion Changelogs

  1. Lead with the benefit - "Faster uploads" beats "Optimized upload handler"
  2. Use emojis for categories - 🚀 Feature, 🐛 Fix, ✨ Improvement, 🔒 Security
  3. Include screenshots - Visuals show what changed
  4. Link to documentation - Help users learn more
  5. Keep it scannable - Use headers, bullets, and bold text

Publishing Your Changelog

Notion allows you to share databases publicly, which is useful for customer-facing changelogs:

How to Make Your Changelog Public

  1. Click Share in the top right of your database
  2. Toggle Share to web to ON
  3. Copy the public URL
  4. Link it from your website's footer or navigation
💡 Pro Tip:

Create a dedicated "Public Changelog" view that only shows entries with Status = "Published". This lets you draft updates internally before making them public.

Embedding in Your Website

You can embed your Notion changelog directly into your website using an iframe:

<iframe 
  src="https://your-domain.notion.site/your-changelog-url"
  width="100%"
  height="800"
  frameborder="0"
  allowfullscreen
></iframe>

However, this approach has limitations (see next section).

Notion Limitations for Changelogs

While Notion is great for getting started, it has some significant limitations as your changelog grows:

Feature Notion Dedicated Tool
Custom domain ❌ Notion URL only ✅ Your domain
RSS/JSON feeds ❌ Not available ✅ Built-in
SEO optimization ❌ Poor indexing ✅ Optimized
Analytics ❌ None ✅ View tracking
In-app widgets ❌ Not possible ✅ Embeddable
GitHub integration ❌ Manual only ✅ Auto-sync
Slack/Discord notifications ❌ Limited ✅ Webhooks
Mobile optimization ❌ Clunky ✅ Responsive

When Notion Becomes Painful

Watch for these warning signs:

  • Customers can't find your changelog (buried in a Notion subdomain)
  • You're manually copying updates to email announcements
  • Your team avoids updating it because the workflow is clunky
  • You want to see which updates get the most engagement
  • You need an in-app notification widget

When to Switch to a Dedicated Changelog Tool

There's a natural progression most teams follow:

Stage 1: Notion (0-100 customers)

You're validating your product. A simple Notion page works fine for early adopters who don't expect polish.

Stage 2: Simple Website (100-1,000 customers)

You've outgrown Notion's limitations. You want a proper domain, better SEO, and basic analytics.

Stage 3: Dedicated Tool (1,000+ customers)

Changelog is a core part of your product communication. You need feeds, widgets, and integrations.

🎯 Our Recommendation

Start with Notion if you're already using it. But plan to migrate to a dedicated tool once you have paying customers and want changelog to be a growth channel, not just an afterthought.

Key Takeaways

  • Notion is great for starting out - Low friction, familiar tool
  • Use a database, not just a page - Properties and views give you organization
  • Write for your audience - Benefits over technical details
  • Plan for the future - Know when it's time to upgrade
  • Dedicated tools offer more - Custom domains, feeds, widgets, and analytics

Whether you stick with Notion or upgrade to a dedicated tool, the most important thing is consistency. A changelog that's updated regularly with valuable information will keep your users engaged and informed—regardless of the platform.
