10 SaaS Changelogs That Drive Engagement

We analyzed 100+ SaaS changelogs to find what works. Here are 10 examples you can learn from, with breakdowns of why they're effective.

Most SaaS changelogs are graveyards of unread updates. But some companies have figured out how to turn their changelogs into engagement engines that drive product adoption, reduce churn, and even generate organic traffic.

We spent two weeks analyzing over 100 SaaS changelogs across different industries and company sizes. The results were stark: the top 10% of changelogs get 10x more engagement than the rest.

What separates the best from the rest? Let's dive into 10 examples that nail it.

What Makes a Changelog Effective?

Before we look at examples, let's establish the criteria we used:

  • Clarity: Can a user understand the value in 10 seconds?
  • Visual appeal: Is it pleasant to read and scan?
  • Personality: Does it sound like a human wrote it?
  • Structure: Is information organized logically?
  • Frequency: Are updates consistent?

The 10 Best SaaS Changelogs

⚡
1. Linear

Linear's changelog is the gold standard for design-forward SaaS companies. Each entry is beautifully formatted with subtle animations and perfect typography. They categorize updates by type (Feature, Improvement, Fix) and always include a "Why this matters" explanation.

What makes it work

They treat each update like a mini-product announcement. Screenshots are crisp, explanations are concise, and the design language matches their main product perfectly.

🎨
2. Figma

Figma's "What's New" modal and changelog strike the perfect balance between comprehensive and digestible. They use video embeds liberally, showing features in action rather than just describing them.

What makes it work

Videos. In a design tool, showing beats telling every time. They also segment updates by user type (designers vs. developers), so everyone sees what's relevant to them.

💬
3. Slack

Slack's release notes read like they were written by a funny, knowledgeable friend. They're casual, use emojis effectively, and always explain the user benefit upfront.

What makes it work

Tone of voice. They don't say "Implemented message threading functionality." They say "You can now reply to messages in threads. Finally, right?"

📊
4. Stripe

Stripe's changelog is exhaustive—and that's the point. Developer tools need comprehensive documentation, and Stripe delivers. Each entry links to detailed documentation.

What makes it work

Depth with navigation. You can scan for headlines or dive deep into API changes. The search functionality is excellent, and they maintain changelogs for every API version.

📝
5. Notion

Notion embeds their changelog directly in their product and uses it as a discovery mechanism for new features. When you open the app after an update, you see what's new.

What makes it work

In-product placement. Users don't need to visit a separate page. This increases visibility dramatically—especially for features that might otherwise go unnoticed.

🚀
6. Vercel

Vercel's changelog combines technical depth with beautiful presentation. They use their own deployment previews to show interactive demos of new features.

What makes it work

Dogfooding. They use their own product to demonstrate updates. Interactive demos let developers see exactly how new features work before implementing them.

🐙
7. GitHub

GitHub's changelog is notable for its transparency. They explain not just what changed, but why. When they deprecate features, they provide detailed migration guides.

What makes it work

Context. GitHub understands that developers need to understand the reasoning behind changes. Their changelogs read like well-reasoned essays, not bullet lists.

🎯
8. Superhuman

Superhuman's changelog emails are legendary. They arrive like clockwork every Friday, summarizing the week's improvements in a scannable, beautifully designed format.

What makes it work

Email distribution. They bring the changelog to users instead of waiting for users to visit. The Friday timing captures weekend planning attention.

☁️
9. Cloudflare

Cloudflare's changelog is exhaustive but well-organized. They use tags extensively, allowing users to subscribe only to updates relevant to their usage.

What makes it work

Granular subscriptions. Users can follow specific products or update types. This reduces noise and ensures subscribers actually read what they receive.

🔧
10. Zapier

Zapier's changelog focuses on integrations—their core value proposition. Each new app integration gets a dedicated entry explaining what users can build with it.

What makes it work

Use-case focus. They don't just announce "New integration: Trello." They show a workflow: "Automatically create Trello cards from Slack messages."

Key Takeaways for Your Changelog

  • Lead with value. Explain why users should care before explaining what changed.
  • Use visuals. Screenshots, GIFs, and videos dramatically increase engagement.
  • Match your voice. Your changelog should sound like your product, not a corporate press release.
  • Make it discoverable. In-product placement or email distribution beats hoping users visit a page.
  • Categorize thoughtfully. Help users filter for what's relevant to them.
  • Be consistent. Regular updates train users to check back.
  • Link to details. Provide depth for users who want it without overwhelming those who don't.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

We also saw plenty of changelogs that miss the mark. Here are the most common pitfalls:

  1. Developer-speak. "Refactored authentication middleware" means nothing to users. Say "You can now log in faster and more securely."
  2. No visuals. Walls of text without images are skipped.
  3. Inconsistent updates. Six months of silence followed by 50 updates is overwhelming.
  4. Missing context. Users need to know if an update affects them.
  5. Burying the lede. Don't make users read three paragraphs to understand what changed.
